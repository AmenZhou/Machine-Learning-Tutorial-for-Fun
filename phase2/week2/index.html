🎯 What is Overfitting?

💡 In plain English

Your model studied the training data too hard — it memorised the answers instead of learning the pattern. It aces every training question but fails on anything new.

The CloudSync Story

CloudSync trained a cost model on months January through August. On training data it scored R² = 0.99 — almost perfect. Then September arrived.

Training (Jan – Aug)
R² = 0.99

Model fits perfectly. Passes every historical data point with tiny error.

September (unseen)
Predicted: $2,100
Actual: $3,580
Off by 70% 🔥
Why? The model memorised the shape of the first 8 months' noise instead of learning that bills grow over time. It had no idea what to do when a new month appeared.

The Two Failure Modes

Overfitting — Too Complex

The model is too wiggly. It memorises every bump and quirk in the training data, including random noise.

Signal: Train R² near 1.0, Test R² much lower.

Underfitting — Too Simple

The model is too rigid. A straight line trying to fit a curved trend misses the pattern entirely.

Signal: Both Train R² and Test R² are low.

The Sweet Spot — Just Right

Train R² and Test R² are both high and close together. The model learned the real pattern, not the noise.

Engineering analogy: Like caching API responses too aggressively — 100% hit rate on known traffic patterns, 0% on new requests you haven't seen before. You need a cache policy, not a cache dump.

From Week 1 to Week 2

From Week 1 you know gradient descent adjusts weights to minimise loss on training data. Now the question is: what if it fits the training data too well? It's not wrong — it's doing exactly what you asked. The problem is you didn't hold back a test set to check whether it generalised.

The Fix: Always Hold Back Test Data

# Split your data BEFORE training. Never touch the test set.
Training set → model learns from this
Test set     → never seen during training — measures real performance

# Rule of thumb: 80% train / 20% test
# Or: train on months 1–8, test on months 9–12

✂️ Split the Data

🎯 Your challenge

Move the split to choose how many months train the model. Watch train R² and test R² — the gap between them is your overfitting signal.


What to Look For

⚠️ Large gap = overfitting

If train R² ≈ 0.99 but test R² ≈ 0.40, the model memorised rather than learned. Adding more training data usually helps.

✅ Small gap = generalising

If train R² and test R² are both high and close, the model learned the real pattern and will work on new months.

🔁 Cross-Validation


  

    
💡 In plain English

    
One split can be unlucky. Cross-validation rotates the test window — every month gets a turn as the test set. The average is far more reliable than any single split.

Why One Split Isn't Enough

    

      If you always test on months 9–12, your reported score depends heavily on whether those months are "easy" or "hard". Cross-validation fixes this by averaging scores across every possible test window.
    

    

      sklearn one-liner: cross_val_score(model, X, y, cv=4) does all 4 folds automatically and returns an array of R² scores. Take the mean.
